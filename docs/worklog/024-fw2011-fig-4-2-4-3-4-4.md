## What changed

### `figures/figutil.jl` (new)

A small `include`-d helper (not a package module) shared by the
pole-field surface figures:

  - `pole_field_lattice(half, N)` — the `N×N` square lattice over
    `[-half,half]²` plus the flat target vector in the order that
    makes `reshape(_, N, N)[i,j]` land on `(xs[i], ys[j])`.
  - `pole_field_figure(xs, ys, Z; title, ucap, …)` — the `Axis3`
    viridis surface + faint wireframe in the MATLAB-`surf`-like view,
    returning `(fig, ax)` so callers can overlay figure-specific
    annotations before saving.

`fw2011_fig_3_1.jl` is refactored onto these two helpers (the
lattice-build and render boilerplate it carried inline is now the
shared path). Behaviour and output unchanged.

### `figures/fw2011_fig_4_2.jl` (new)

Two-panel real-axis line plot. For each of the four distinct initial
slopes it calls `path_network_solve` on a 1-D grid of 241 real-axis
target points over `x ∈ [-10.5, 1.5]`, takes `real(u)` clipped to
`±50`, and plots against FW's `[-10,2]×[-3,2]` framing with the
`±√(-x/6)` leading-term branches dotted. Panel (a) draws the three
bracketing curves; panel (b) the two tronquée cases.

### `figures/fw2011_fig_4_3.jl`, `figures/fw2011_fig_4_4.jl` (new)

`|u(z)|` pole-field surfaces over `[-10,10]²` (121×121 lattice,
`path_network_solve` with Schwarz symmetry, `h = 0.5`, `order = 30`),
built via `figutil.jl`. Each overlays FW's two annotations (md:233):
the thick real-axis `|u(x)|` line traced on the surface, and the
short imaginary-axis cross-line marking the origin. The two scripts
differ only in `UP0` (`1.8518` vs `1.8519`).

No `src/` or `test/` change.

## Frictions

  - **`solve_pade` integrates one direction only.** Fig 4.2's curves
    run down the *negative* real axis, but `solve_pade`'s driver loop
    is `while state.z < z_end` — it cannot step from `z = 0` toward
    `z = -10`. Rather than an `x ↦ -s` change of variables plus a
    second forward `solve_pade`, the cleaner route — and the one FW
    actually uses (md:233: the real-axis curves *are* the thick lines
    on the Fig 4.3/4.4 path-network surfaces) — is `path_network_solve`
    on a 1-D real-axis target grid. It detours around each pole in
    the complex plane and never has to step onto one.
  - **The wedge walk always makes net progress, so it reaches
    on-pole targets.** A worry going in: for a target sitting on a
    pole, would the `:min_u` rule refuse to step closer (all five
    candidates have huge `|u|`)? No — the five wedge directions are
    all within `±45°` of the goal direction, so every candidate has a
    positive component toward the target; the walk converges to
    within `h` regardless of `|u|`, and Stage 2 then evaluates the
    local Padé at the on-pole target (large but finite). This is why
    the Fig 4.2 real-axis grid works even where it lands on poles —
    and the same mechanism is what makes the Fig 4.3/4.4 lattices
    cover their on-pole cells.
