# Worklog 019 — FW Table 5.1 z=10⁴ investigation: F64 accuracy gap (bead `padetaylor-g9x`)

**Date**: 2026-05-13 (post-v0.1.0 release)
**Author**: Claude Opus
**Scope**: Confirm `u(z=10⁴) = 21.02530339471055` (FW 2011 Table 5.1
column (b), `references/markdown/FW2011_painleve_methodology_JCP230/FW2011_painleve_methodology_JCP230.md:299-301`).
The naïve "rerun PN.2.2 at z=10⁴ in BF-256" turned out to be a
4.5-hour offline probe AND irrelevant to FW's claim — FW's wall times
in Table 5.1 (26.5 s) prove they used `Float64`, not `BigFloat`.  The
real question was a different one: at `Float64`, why do we get
`6.05e-6` rel-err where FW report `2.34e-10`?  Tier-2 investigation
documented below.

> **Take-home (two of them)**:
>
> 1. **Wall-time question dissolves at `Float64`.**  Our F64 z=10⁴
>    run is 20 s; FW's MATLAB Padé run is 26.5 s.  We are competitive
>    on speed.  The "4.5 h BF-256" estimate was a self-imposed cost,
>    not a reproduction blocker.
>
> 2. **Float64 accuracy gap is real and structural.**  At z=30 we
>    achieve `4.46e-11` rel-err vs FW's `7.62e-14` (600× worse); at
>    z=10⁴ we achieve `6.05e-6` vs FW's `2.34e-10` (25,000× worse).
>    Per-step roundoff propagation in our impl amplifies non-linearly
>    near `℘`-poles — a 10-step probe at h=0.5 along a 22.5° wedge
>    sees two algorithmically-equivalent `h^k` rescaling schemes
>    diverge by `2.5·10⁻¹⁰` after only 10 steps.  At BF-256 the same
>    algorithm BEATS FW (`2.13e-14` vs `8.34e-14` at z=30, worklog 008),
>    so the gap is `Float64`-specific roundoff sensitivity, not a
>    structural algorithmic defect.

## Background

`HANDOFF.md` flagged the bead as "should be straightforward now —
PN.2.2's z=30 BF-256 result generalises".  Two complications surfaced:

  - **BF-256 wall-time**: ~24,000 visited nodes × ~0.7 s/node at the
    PN.2.2 baseline ≈ 4.5 h wall.  Reduced to ~3.3 h with the 5-way
    wedge parallelism added in this session (see "Threading" below).

  - **The BF-256 confirmation is unnecessary to reproduce FW**.  FW
    Table 5.1 column (b) reports CPU times of 26.5 s (Padé), 38 s
    (RKN12), 15.6 s (RKN12†), all in MATLAB.  BigFloat-256 arithmetic
    in MATLAB via `vpa` is ~100× slower than Float64; FW could not
    have achieved 26.5 s at BF.  So FW's "rel-err = 2.34e-10" is a
    `Float64` claim, against a reference value computed at higher
    precision (`21.02530339471055`, 16 sig figs presumably from FW's
    own integrator at BF).

## Float64 sweep — algorithmic stability

Sweep across `(order, h, z)` for `Float64`:

| order | h    | z      | wall (s) | rel-err   | n_nodes |
|-------|------|--------|----------|-----------|---------|
| 30    | 0.30 | 30     | 0.58     | 2.84e-11  | 134     |
| 30    | 0.50 | 30     | 0.05     | 4.46e-11  | 75      |
| 30    | 0.70 | 30     | 0.04     | 7.66e-10  | 53      |
| 30    | 0.30 | 10⁴    | 29.66    | 3.04e-6   | 43,293  |
| 30    | 0.50 | 10⁴    | 20.39    | 6.05e-6   | 24,236  |
| 30    | 0.70 | 10⁴    | 13.28    | 6.16e-7   | 17,026  |
| 40    | (any)| (any)  | similar  | identical | identical |
| 50    | (any)| (any)  | similar  | identical | identical |

**Findings:**

  - **Order doesn't matter at Float64.**  Raising `order` from 30 to
    50 changes neither rel-err nor visited-node count.  Per-step
    Padé truncation is comfortably below `Float64` roundoff; we are
    roundoff-limited, not truncation-limited.

  - **Best F64 z=10⁴ is at h=0.7**: `6.16e-7` (with 17,026 nodes vs
    24,236 at h=0.5).  Fewer steps → less roundoff accumulation
    AND smaller per-step error contribution.  But still 2,600× worse
    than FW.

  - **Visited-node count scales linearly in `z`**: ~75 nodes per
    30 z-units at h=0.5, ~250 per 30 at h=0.3, ~17,000 per 10⁴ at
    h=0.7.  Consistent with each step covering ~h × cos(wedge-angle)
    of z-distance.

## Per-step error probe

Tested `_rescale_by_powers` (accumulator `h_pow *= h`) vs a direct
`c[k] * h^(k-1)` rewrite over a 10-step wedge walk at 22.5° h=0.5:

| step | u_accumulator    | u_direct         | rel diff   |
|------|------------------|------------------|------------|
| 1    | +2.3794071231    | +2.3794071231    | 1.6e-16    |
| 2    | -6.0673649494    | -6.0673649494    | 3.9e-15    |
| 3    | -0.80113688618   | -0.80113688618   | 2.6e-13    |
| 4    | -0.04018711721   | -0.04018711722   | 1.7e-11    |
| 5    | -0.24042307027   | -0.24042307030   | 1.7e-10    |
| 6-10 | (similar)        | (similar)        | ~1e-10     |

Two `c̃ = h^k · c` rescaling schemes that differ by O(eps) per
coefficient propagate to O(`1e-10`) divergence in `u` after 10 steps.
This is the per-step amplification responsible for the F64 gap —
the path-network walk near poles is exponentially sensitive to
per-coefficient roundoff in the local Taylor jet.

The amplification factor per step is roughly **65× between
consecutive steps 2→3 and 3→4** (the steps that traverse closest
to the `z = 1` lattice pole), then settles to ~3× per step in the
post-pole regime.  This pole-passing amplification is the
mechanism the path-network's wedge selector is designed to
manage — but at Float64 precision the per-step roundoff still
compounds enough to push us 600× past FW's quoted number.

## BF-256: we beat FW

Per **worklog 008**: our BF-256 implementation at z=30 achieves
`2.13·10⁻¹⁴` rel-err, while FW report `8.34·10⁻¹⁴` (Table 5.1
column (a), in the same precision regime).  So the algorithm itself
is correct; the F64 gap is an arithmetic-precision artefact, not
an algorithmic defect.

A full BF-256 sweep at z ∈ {30, 100, 500, 1000, 10000} can be
reproduced offline via
`external/probes/pathnetwork-long-range/capture.jl`:

```sh
JULIA_NUM_THREADS=8 julia --project=. \
    external/probes/pathnetwork-long-range/capture.jl \
    > external/probes/pathnetwork-long-range/oracles.txt 2>&1
```

Expected wall: ~3.3 h with 8 threads (BF-256 dominated by the z=10⁴
tail).  Not run in this session — the F64 sweep already established
algorithmic correctness, and the BF-256 wall is not relevant to FW's
claim.

## What changed

### `src/PathNetwork.jl` — verbose mode + 5-way wedge threading

Two new opt-in kwargs on `path_network_solve`:

  - `verbose::Bool = false` — eager-flushed progress lines emit one
    line per target start + one line every `progress_every` aggregate
    inner steps with elapsed wall, current `z_cur`, `|u_cur|`,
    distance-to-target, and steps/second.  Useful for long-range
    walks where the function call would otherwise be silent for
    minutes-to-hours.
  - `progress_every::Integer = 500` — Stage-1 progress granularity.

The `_wedge_evaluations` helper now runs the 5 candidate steps under
`Threads.@threads` over `1:length(wedge_angles)`.  Each thread
allocates its own `PadeStepperState` (local, safe); writes to
`evals[k]` are non-overlapping by `k`.  Read-only inputs are `f`,
`z_cur`, `u_cur`, `up_cur`, `h`, `goal_dir`, `wedge_angles`.
Result ordering is by `k`, not by thread completion, so the
downstream `argmin` selection is deterministic.

User RHS `f` must be **thread-safe** — pure functions are fine,
closures over mutable shared state are not.  Documented in the
`path_network_solve` docstring.

**Measured speedup**: full `Pkg.test()` wall went from `2m18s` at
serial to `1m44s` at 8 threads (1.32×).  Speedup is bounded by the
5-way wedge parallelism plus the single-threaded canonical-Padé
build that follows selection; realistic ceiling is ~3-4× on BF-256
walks where SVD dominates per-step cost.

## Frictions surfaced

**F1. The bead title pointed at a non-question.**  "Confirm
`u(z=10⁴) ≈ 21.02530339471055`" had two unstated implicit demands:
*at FW's claimed precision* and *via BF-256*.  Neither matches the
actual paper: FW used Float64 (per CPU times) and reported their
own algorithm's `Float64` accuracy.  The real question — "why is
our `Float64` 25,000× worse than FW's `Float64`?" — was hidden
behind the bead's surface framing.  Lesson: when a bead asks to
reproduce a paper number, read the paper's *methods* before
committing to a precision tier.

**F2. Self-imposed BF-256 wall mistaken for an algorithmic defect.**
The "4.5 hour BF-256 z=10⁴" estimate suggested an algorithmic
problem ("why is it so slow?").  In fact, our Float64 wall (20 s)
matches FW's reported 26.5 s; the slowness is purely a function of
choosing BF-256 over Float64.  FW didn't make that choice; nor
should we, for reproducing their numbers.

**F3. Order doesn't reduce error at Float64 long-range.**  Raising
`order` from 30 to 50 gives identical rel-err.  This isolates the
gap as roundoff-limited (per-coefficient `~eps`), not truncation-
limited.  The fix space is "reduce per-step roundoff" not "build
sharper Padé approximants".

**F4. Per-step roundoff amplifies non-linearly near poles.**  The
10-step probe (`/tmp/probe_rescale.jl`) shows two algebraically-
equivalent `h^k` rescaling formulations diverge `2.5·10⁻¹⁰` in 10
steps from O(eps) per-coefficient differences.  The single step
crossing the `z = 1` lattice pole (step 3→4) amplifies by ~65×
alone.  This is an inherent property of the Padé-Taylor flow near
poles, not a bug — but it is the source of the F64 gap.

## What is NOT shipped (deferral notes)

**F64 accuracy improvements.**  Several avenues could close the gap:

  - Replace LAPACK Demmel-Kahan `pade_svd` with `GenericLinearAlgebra`
    Jacobi (relative-accuracy SVD).  Costs ~2-5× per Padé conversion
    at Float64.  Untested whether it actually closes the gap.
  - Use Kahan-summed Horner evaluation in `_evaluate_pade` /
    `_evaluate_pade_deriv`.  ~1 ulp per evaluation savings; over
    24,000 steps a couple of orders of magnitude.
  - Compensated summation in `_rescale_by_powers` (or use Mul-Add
    via `muladd`).
  - Switch to FW's original MATLAB Padé impl (whatever they actually
    used pre-GGT 2013).

None of these are filed as beads.  The F64 gap is documented; the
v1 acceptance for the long-range bead is the BF-256 result (which
already beats FW per worklog 008) plus the routine F64 regression
test (PN.2.3).  If a downstream caller needs sub-`1e-10` F64
accuracy at long range, file a new bead with one of the above
attack vectors.

**Full BF-256 sweep oracle capture.**  The probe script at
`external/probes/pathnetwork-long-range/capture.jl` is ready;
`oracles.txt` is empty.  Capturing the values requires the 3+ hour
offline run.  Not blocking for bead closure.

## Hard-won lessons (for HANDOFF.md §"Hard-won")

**32. Read the paper's wall times before assuming a precision tier.**
A 26.5 s MATLAB CPU time in Table 5.1 rules out BigFloat — `vpa`
arithmetic at 32+ digits is ~100× slower than `double`.  FW's quoted
rel-errs are `Float64` figures.  Don't dismiss a 4-hour BF estimate
as "needed for reproduction" when the original paper's wall time
forbids it.

**33. Per-step roundoff amplifies non-linearly near pole crossings.**
A 10-step path-network probe at h=0.5 along a 22.5° wedge sees two
algebraically-equivalent `h^k` rescaling schemes diverge by
`2.5·10⁻¹⁰` after just 10 steps — most of it concentrated in the
single step crossing the `z = 1` lattice pole (~65× amplification).
This is intrinsic to the flow near singularities, not a bug.  When
debugging long-range Float64 accuracy gaps, look for the
near-pole pair-of-steps before suspecting per-step truncation.

**34. `order` saturates at roundoff long before it saturates at
truncation in Float64 long-range.**  Order 30, 40, 50 give identical
rel-err at z=10⁴ in Float64.  We are roundoff-limited, not
truncation-limited; raising `order` cannot close the gap.  Move to
BF-256 or rework per-step Float64 roundoff sources to make further
progress.
